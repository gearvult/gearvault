## What Makes a Great Digital Piano

The three things that separate a $500 keyboard from a $1,500 digital piano:

**Key Action** - How the keys feel under your fingers. At this price, you should get fully weighted, graded hammer action that feels like a real acoustic piano. Heavier in the bass, lighter in the treble.

**Sound Engine** - The best digital pianos sample real concert grands note-by-note, including the subtle resonances, sympathetic vibrations, and decay characteristics. You should hear the difference between a soft touch and a hard strike on every note.

**Speaker System** - If you're playing at home without an external amp, the built-in speakers need to fill a room and reproduce bass frequencies accurately.

## FAQ

**Q: Should I buy a digital piano or an acoustic?**
A: Under $3,000, a digital piano will give you a better playing experience than an acoustic at the same price. Acoustics need regular tuning (~$150/year), are heavy, and can't be played silently with headphones. Digital pianos require zero maintenance.

**Q: Do I need 88 keys?**
A: For piano playing, yes. All models in this list have 88 weighted keys. Keyboards with fewer keys or semi-weighted action are better categorized as MIDI controllers or synths, not digital pianos.

**Q: How long do digital pianos last?**
A: 10-20+ years with normal use. They don't go out of tune, and the key mechanisms are designed for millions of presses.

**Q: Can I connect these to a computer for recording?**
A: Yes, all models have USB output. They appear as MIDI controllers in any DAW (Logic, Ableton, FL Studio, etc.). Some also output audio via USB.
