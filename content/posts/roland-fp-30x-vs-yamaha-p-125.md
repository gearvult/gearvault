## Quick Verdict

The **Roland FP-30X** (~$699) is the better piano with more features, better speakers, and Bluetooth MIDI. The **Yamaha P-145** (~$460) is the better value if you're on a tight budget. Both have excellent weighted key actions suitable for learning and performing.

## Why These Two Pianos

These are the two best-selling beginner digital pianos in the world, and for good reason. Both deliver real piano feel and sound at accessible prices. If you're a student, returning player, or starting your piano journey, your decision is almost certainly between these two.

### What Makes It Special

Roland's SuperNATURAL sound engine models piano sound in real-time rather than playing back samples. The result is more natural velocity response - the transition from soft to loud feels seamless rather than stepping between recorded layers.

The PHA-4 action has escapement simulation, which recreates the "click" you feel on an acoustic piano when pressing a key very slowly. This tactile feedback is important for developing proper technique.

Bluetooth MIDI lets you connect wirelessly to apps like Simply Piano, Flowkey, or any DAW on your iPad. No cables needed - just pair and play.

### Pros
- SuperNATURAL modeling sounds more natural than samples
- PHA-4 action with escapement feels realistic
- Bluetooth MIDI for wireless app connectivity
- Dual headphone jacks (great for teacher/student)
- 56 tones including electric pianos, strings, organs
- Better speaker system than the Yamaha

### Cons
- $240 more expensive than the Yamaha P-145
- 31 lbs - manageable but not ultralight
- No Bluetooth audio (only MIDI)
- Speakers adequate but not room-filling

### What Makes It Special

The Yamaha P-145 replaced the legendary P-125, which was the best-selling digital piano in the world for years. The GHC (Graded Hammer Compact) action is heavier in the bass and lighter in the treble, mimicking an acoustic piano.

Yamaha samples their own CFIIIS concert grand - a $150,000+ instrument. The piano sound is rich, full, and instantly recognizable as "Yamaha." For many pianists, this is THE piano sound.

At 24.6 lbs and $460, the P-145 is the most accessible quality digital piano on the market.

### Pros
- $240 cheaper than the Roland
- Yamaha concert grand samples sound gorgeous
- Only 24.6 lbs - ultraportable
- GHC action feels great for the price
- Yamaha reliability and resale value
- Smart Pianist app for easy control

### Cons
- Only 10 tones (vs 56 on the Roland)
- Smaller 7W speakers (less volume)
- No Bluetooth MIDI
- Single headphone jack (no teacher/student dual)
- Sample-based (less dynamic range than Roland's modeling)

## Who Should Buy Which

### Buy the Roland FP-30X if:
- You're serious about learning and plan to play for years
- You want Bluetooth for app-based learning (Simply Piano, Flowkey)
- You need dual headphone jacks (teacher/student or late-night duo)
- You want more than just piano sounds (organs, strings, electric pianos)
- The $240 difference fits your budget

### Buy the Yamaha P-145 if:
- Budget is the primary concern
- You mainly need a great piano sound (the Yamaha CFIIIS is beautiful)
- Portability matters (6 lbs lighter)
- You're buying for a child who may or may not stick with it
- You prefer Yamaha's piano tone (many pianists do)

## FAQ

**Q: Are these good enough for grade exams?**
A: Yes. Both have fully weighted 88-key actions that satisfy exam requirements (ABRSM, RCM, etc.) up to at least Grade 8.

**Q: Do I need a stand and pedal?**
A: The pianos come with a music rest and sustain pedal. A proper X-stand (~$30-50) is recommended. Optional furniture stands with triple pedal units are available for both (~$150-200).

**Q: Can I connect these to a computer for recording?**
A: Yes, both have USB-MIDI output. Plug into your computer and use any DAW or virtual instrument.

**Q: Will I outgrow these quickly?**
A: The FP-30X will last most players 5-10 years. The P-145 might feel limiting after 2-3 years of serious study due to fewer features, but the piano sound and action remain excellent.
