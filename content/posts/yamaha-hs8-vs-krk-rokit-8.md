## The 8-Inch Monitor Question

If you're looking at 8-inch monitors, you're serious about your sound. 8-inch woofers deliver deeper, more accurate bass than their 5-inch counterparts, making them ideal for:

- Mixing bass-heavy music without a subwoofer
- Working in rooms larger than 120 square feet
- Critical listening where you need to hear below 40Hz

The Yamaha HS8 and KRK Rokit 8 G4 are the two most popular 8-inch monitors under $400 each. Here's how they compare.

The midrange is where the HS8 truly excels. Vocals, guitars, snares - everything in the 200Hz-5kHz range is reproduced with surgical precision. This is critical because the midrange is where 80% of mixing decisions happen.

This isn't "wrong" - it's a design choice. KRK voices their monitors for the genres their users predominantly make. The G4's built-in DSP lets you flatten this out if you want, but most KRK users like the character.

The 203W amplification is significantly more powerful than the Yamaha's 120W, making the Rokits louder and punchier at high volumes. If you push them in a bigger room, they maintain composure better.

The G4's killer feature is the built-in DSP room correction with an LCD display on the back panel. You get 25 EQ presets tailored for common room problems: corner placement, desk reflections, wall proximity. The Yamaha only offers basic high/low trim switches.

For untreated rooms, this DSP can partially compensate for acoustic issues that would otherwise ruin your monitoring accuracy.

## The Genre Factor

### If You Make Hip-Hop, Trap, or EDM
**Get the KRK Rokit 8 G4.** The bass emphasis helps you craft your low end with confidence. You'll feel the 808s and sub-bass as you work, which is essential for these genres. The DSP room correction is also a big deal if your studio is a bedroom.

### If You Mix Rock, Pop, Jazz, or Classical
**Get the Yamaha HS8.** Flat response means your mixes translate accurately to every playback system. The honest midrange helps you nail vocal levels and EQ. These genres live in the midrange, and the HS8 is the midrange king.

### If You Do Both
**Get the Yamaha HS8** and check your low end on headphones. It's easier to add bass mentally when mixing on flat monitors than to subtract it when mixing on hyped ones.

## Room Considerations

Both of these are 8-inch monitors with powerful bass output. Your room needs to be at least **120-150 square feet** to handle them properly. In a small bedroom (under 100 sq ft), 8-inch monitors will create bass buildup in corners that makes mixing inaccurate regardless of which pair you choose.

**If your room is small:** Get the 5-inch versions instead (Yamaha HS5 or KRK Rokit 5 G4) and add a subwoofer later.

**If your room is untreated:** The KRK's DSP room correction gives it an advantage. But spending $200 on acoustic panels will improve your monitoring more than any DSP.

## FAQ

**Q: Can I mix bass music on the Yamaha HS8?**
A: Absolutely. Flat monitors don't mean "no bass" - they mean accurate bass. You can mix bass music on HS8s; you just need to trust your meters and check on headphones/other systems.

**Q: Will the KRK's bass emphasis ruin my mixes?**
A: Not if you're aware of it. Learn how your monitors translate by checking mixes on other systems (car, earbuds, phone). Over time, you'll mentally calibrate.

**Q: Do I need an amplifier for these?**
A: No. Both are active (powered) monitors with built-in amplifiers. Just plug into your audio interface and go.

**Q: How far should they be from the wall?**
A: At least 8-12 inches. Both have rear bass ports, so wall proximity increases bass. The Yamaha's room control switches can help compensate; the KRK's DSP presets include wall-placement modes.
